# Birth of Rome Game Design

## Design Claim

Cities are not built by maximizing one resource. Rome emerges when terrain, household production, ritual confidence, military obligation, exchange, and public authority reinforce one another without allowing any one system to consume the rest.

## First Vertical Slice

The playable build covers four complete acts and a regional-planning epilogue:

1. **Hill Settlements**: cooperation among defensible communities, household production, water, and the river crossing.
2. **City of Kings**: sustained drainage, specialized craft, public markets, concentrated authority, and monumental construction.
3. **Early Republic**: annual magistrates, Senate continuity, assembly consent, debt and levy pressure, secession, Veii, soldier pay, settlement after conquest, and preparation for the Gallic crisis.
4. **Sack and Reconstruction**: proportional crisis losses, competing recovery doctrines, debt and land claims, selected Tier IV rebuilding, and the Latin settlement of 338 BC.
5. **Regional Planning**: differentiated political statuses, roads, colonies, obligations, hostile access, and overextension through 304 BC.

## Turn Loop

1. Inspect stores, civic measures, and political support.
2. Select a terrain district.
3. Allocate the available workforce among fields, public works, and levy service.
4. Establish works while materials and population-derived construction capacity permit.
5. Continue, pause, repair, upgrade, or clear existing works.
6. Resolve the council when one is called.
7. End the season and absorb production, upkeep, structural pressure, damage, and any event.
8. At turn five, enter the City of Kings and unlock Tier II forms.
9. After the turn-ten judgment, continue through the 509 BC transition into constrained republican offices and selected Tier III works.
10. Carry the Republic through Veii and finish at 390 BC with a recorded Gallic readiness posture for Act IV.
11. Enter the damaged city, choose emergency priorities and a reconstruction doctrine, then carry its consequences into the Latin settlement.
12. Keep the city as the construction center while using a separate regional map to assign compacts, road crews, settlers, stores, and garrisons.

## Regional Strategy Spine

The regional map is not a territory painter. Each community retains a defined relationship with Rome: citizens, partial citizens, Latin allies, treaty allies, or a hostile power. Military contribution, local autonomy, trade access, and revolt risk remain separate, so no single incorporation meter can erase the political tradeoff.

Road projects consume the same seasonal public capacity and city resources as urban works. Completed corridors improve supply, exchange, and response but also increase hostile access. Colonies remove population from Rome, spend grain, treasury, and bronze, and add a garrison commitment. City granaries, markets, defenses, stores, and treasury determine whether these obligations create strategic depth or overextension.

The three reference doctrines establish distinct viable baselines: fortified strategic depth, commercial corridors, and reciprocal alliance. Their dedicated regional score combines political balance, supportable commitments, infrastructure, and trade rather than rewarding raw map coverage.

## Republican Government

The Republic is a separate state system, not a renamed monarchy. Annual office modes cap public-works capacity. Senate standing represents continuity, counsel, finance, cult, and elite coordination rather than a modern legislature. Assembly consent measures whether public commands retain support among the citizen body. Debt strain and levy burden rise when extended service removes households from field work; reserves and limited service terms can reduce that pressure. Tribunes cannot appear during the 509 settlement and become available only at the traditional first-secession stage in 494 BC.

## War Commitments

Act III tracks pressure on Veii, siege persistence, soldier pay, veteran cohesion, land expectations, and the Gallic threat separately from the city and constitution. Levy allocation can sustain a siege but also removes household labor. Soldier pay moves part of that burden to public accounts. Victory creates competing claims over territory, tribute, cult, and rewards. The final crisis score combines readiness, veterans, defenses, food reserves, debt, levy burden, institutional support, and the player\'s chosen posture. It records preparation for Act IV without pretending the disputed sack has already resolved.

## Sack and Reconstruction

The Act III judgment remains separate from Act IV destruction. Entering reconstruction converts the recorded readiness posture into district-specific damage, population loss, depleted stores, damaged works, displacement, fire exposure, broken records, and wall urgency. Prepared cities still suffer; exposed cities retain a citadel, institutions, and surviving district identity rather than receiving a scripted total reset.

Emergency relief precedes a persistent doctrine. Rapid rebuilding reduces devastation and displacement quickly but carries fire and circulation problems. Planned rebuilding spends more and returns households more slowly while improving records and exposure. Militarized recovery reduces wall urgency and raises readiness while delaying shelter and increasing levy burdens. Debt, public labor, and land claims then shape the final Latin settlement.

## District Networks

Districts are linked rather than isolated. Housing benefits from nearby water and sacred precincts. Markets produce only half their treasury output without connected storage or an improved road, with each service restoring one quarter. Tier II housing receives only 55 percent of its added capacity without water, drainage, and road access, with each service restoring 15 percent. Workshops benefit from water; defenses benefit from craft. The build dock exposes services, output, capacity, and placement bonuses before confirmation. The map can separately display terrain, road links, water reach, drainage, and defended approaches. Tier II water, drainage, and defense works extend service into neighboring districts.

## District Risk

Fire, disease, and flood exposure are calculated by district. Occupancy density, housing, markets, workshops, terrain, water coverage, and drainage coverage all contribute to visible risk scores. Disease exposure affects projected illness. Severe fire exposure wears vulnerable housing, workshop, and market structures. The turn-four flood resolves from the Forum and Tiber Bank risk values, so drainage and restrained floodplain occupation materially reduce damage and losses without revealing the event in advance.

## Households and Population

Population occupies district-specific housing rather than a single abstract city pool. Baseline settlement capacity comes from the occupied hills; housing works expand the district where they are built. Food, water, shelter, sanitation, and order determine births, arrivals, departures, and illness. Known causes are projected before season resolution, while floods and enemy action add losses only when the event occurs.

The population ledger separates households, dependents, available workers, levy-eligible people, and craftsmen. Shrine service, festivals, and temple rites reserve a visible share of eligible people before the remainder is divided among farming, public works, and levy service. Farming changes grain yield, public works determines action capacity, and levy rotation changes readiness. The interface identifies the district and political interest behind ritual and project burdens. No labor resource can be accumulated independently of the people who provide it.

## Major Projects

Ordinary works complete within one season. The Cloaca Works, Temple of Jupiter, and stone gates require multiple assigned work seasons. Materials are committed when a project begins, the site remains occupied, and civic effects apply only at completion. Projects can pause without losing construction progress, but repeatedly abandoning them damages confidence in public obligations.

## Condition and Replacement

Flood and military events damage specific districts. Damaged buildings lose part or all of their seasonal production and eventually create an order penalty. Repair competes with expansion for seasonal capacity. Clearing a work reverses its civic effects and returns only limited material, preventing demolition from becoming a free respecification system.

## Historical Boundaries

The campaign is a model, not a claim that every named council occurred. Roman literary tradition, archaeological evidence, and designer inference must remain visibly distinct. The chronology of royal drainage works, ethnic labels, and exact early institutions remains uncertain.
