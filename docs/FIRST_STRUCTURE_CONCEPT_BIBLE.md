# First Structure Concept Bible

## Purpose

This document defines what the first sixteen building renderings should depict before any image is generated. It separates archaeological evidence from visual inference, gives each asset a distinctive silhouette, and provides rejection criteria. The render artist should be able to sketch each structure from these descriptions without inventing a second art direction.

The approved **Palatine Huts** concept becomes the master reference for camera, light, scale, ground contact, material texture, and transparent-edge treatment.

## Shared Scene Rules

- Use the locked three-quarter orthographic camera from the art specification: 35 degrees around the structure and 28 degrees above it.
- Treat one asset as a small civic site, not necessarily one isolated building. Housing, markets, storage, craft, and defense should show the minimum surrounding features needed to explain their function.
- Keep all permanent structures one story high in this batch. Height comes from roofs, palisades, gateworks, and terrain rather than later monumental massing.
- Use dark gray and reddish local tufa selectively, not white marble. Timber should be irregular but purposefully joined. Walls may use wattle-and-daub, packed earth, rough stone footings, or early mud brick.
- Use straw and thatch heavily in the earliest assets. Terracotta roof tile may begin appearing in the upgraded structures, but it should not cover every roof.
- A few small tools, vessels, bundles, or animals can explain scale. Do not fill the frame with people or narrative action.
- Every asset must retain a recognizable outer silhouette at 96 x 96 pixels.

## Evidence Labels

- **Evidence-led:** directly anchored to surviving traces, official archaeological reconstruction, or well-attested material practice.
- **Type inference:** a plausible functional reconstruction where early Roman evidence does not preserve one exact building.
- **Game abstraction:** several related facilities combined into one legible game asset.

## 1. Palatine Huts

**Previous game label:** Round Huts
**Approved display label:** Palatine Huts
**Evidence status:** Evidence-led cluster with limited free reconstruction around the principal hut.

### Mental Image

Three low, straw-roofed dwellings sit on a scuffed tufa hilltop: one dominant oval or rounded-rectangular single-room hut with a tiny two-post entrance porch, flanked by two smaller household structures and an outdoor work area.

### Form and Construction

- The main hut has a modest footprint, approximately 5 by 3.5 meters, rather than a large communal hall.
- Its wall line is oval or rectangular with softened corners. Vertical timber posts form the frame; woven wattles fill the gaps beneath a thin, uneven layer of pale earthen daub.
- A steep straw roof descends low over the walls. A subtle ridge or central support should suggest the roof structure without exposing it.
- The short entrance wall carries a shallow portico supported by two posts. The opening is dark and small, with no hinged monumental door.
- A faint thread of hearth smoke may escape through the thatch or doorway. Do not add a built chimney.
- The two secondary huts vary slightly in plan and roof repair, preventing the cluster from reading as duplicated models.
- Add one low storage rack, two ceramic vessels, a split-log bench, and a narrow runoff groove cut into the ground.

### Color and Wear

Straw ranges from muted gold to gray-brown. Daub is pale clay with darker wattle visible where weather has worn it. Posts are smoke-darkened near the doorway. The tufa ground should remain a restrained gray-brown so the roofs carry the silhouette.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The player must immediately see **a cluster of household huts**, not one barn or a Celtic roundhouse.

### Reject If

- The huts are perfect circles with conical roofs.
- The scene has stone chimneys, glass windows, tiled streets, or a fenced medieval village.
- Every structure is identical.
- The main dwelling looks spacious, symmetrical, or monumental.

## 2. Timber Shrine

**Evidence status:** Type inference grounded in early open-air sacred boundaries and perishable Italic construction.

### Mental Image

A carefully marked sacred enclosure surrounds a low stone altar and votive post, with a small open-front timber canopy protecting ritual objects at the rear.

### Form and Construction

- The enclosure is the primary form: a roughly rectangular sacred boundary made from low posts, rope, stones, or a shallow cut in the earth.
- At the center sits a waist-high rough tufa altar with ash staining and a shallow offering dish.
- Behind it is a modest timber canopy, not a complete temple. Four posts support a slightly pitched thatch or simple terracotta-edged roof.
- A rear wattle screen protects a shelf holding two small ceramic vessels and a bronze or wooden votive object.
- One upright pole or forked sacred marker provides vertical emphasis without becoming a statue.
- The ground inside is swept and lighter than the path outside, making ritual separation visible from above.

### Color and Wear

Use pale ash, warm timber, darkened altar stone, and only a small terracotta or faded red accent. The site should feel maintained rather than wealthy.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The altar, boundary, and canopy must form a clear triangular composition that reads as **a consecrated place**.

### Reject If

- It resembles a miniature marble, Doric, Ionic, or Corinthian temple.
- It has a large anthropomorphic cult statue, grand stairs, or a high podium.
- The sacred boundary is absent and the asset reads as an ordinary hut.

## 3. Shallow Well

**Evidence status:** Type inference.

### Mental Image

A small hand-dug well with a low rough-stone curb, forked timber uprights, a rope and clay-weighted bucket, surrounded by two water jars and a worn patch of dry ground.

### Form and Construction

- The circular opening is the dominant shape, approximately 1.2 meters across.
- A low curb of irregular tufa blocks keeps loose soil and runoff out of the shaft.
- Two forked posts carry a simple horizontal lifting pole. Use rope over the pole rather than an elaborate geared windlass.
- Show one clay or leather bucket resting at the curb and two large jars nearby.
- Add a short drainage groove leading away from the drawing side and damp staining only immediately around the curb.
- The asset must sit on dry or elevated ground; it should never look placed in the marshy Forum basin.

### Color and Wear

Gray tufa, sun-bleached timber, dark rope, buff pottery, and a small patch of cool damp earth.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The ring-shaped curb and crossbar must remain recognizable as **a well**, distinct from the later covered cistern.

### Reject If

- It has a decorative stone pavilion, tiled roof, iron crank, or deep masonry tower.
- It looks like a fountain or aqueduct outlet.
- Standing water surrounds the site.

## 4. Drainage Ditch

**Evidence status:** Game abstraction informed by the need to drain and fill the Forum valley.

### Mental Image

An elongated open channel cuts across wet low ground, its sides held by timber stakes and rough stone, with a plank crossing and several smaller feeder cuts carrying brown water toward it.

### Form and Construction

- Use a long diagonal footprint to distinguish infrastructure from a building.
- The main ditch is broad enough for one worker to stand in, with sloped muddy sides and a visible shallow flow.
- Timber stakes, woven brush revetment, and occasional rough stones stabilize the most fragile edges.
- Two narrow feeder channels enter from different angles, making the drainage function obvious.
- A simple two-plank foot crossing provides scale and shows that the channel divides movement even while improving the ground.
- Spoil from the cut forms a low dry bank along one side. Add bundled brush and a wooden shovel or mattock, but no active crew.

### Color and Wear

Wet umber soil, muted green reeds, dark shallow water, gray timber, and lighter excavated earth.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The branching channel must read as **managed drainage**, not a decorative stream.

### Reject If

- The channel is vaulted, fully stone-lined, or large enough to be the completed Cloaca Works.
- It appears clean, symmetrical, or paved.
- The asset is a freestanding building.

## 5. Cattle Market

**Evidence status:** Game abstraction tied to river, salt, livestock, and exchange routes.

### Mental Image

Two timber livestock pens open toward a trampled bargaining yard beside a small weighing shelter, with a pair of cattle, salt baskets, tethering posts, and merchants' jars defining the exchange.

### Form and Construction

- The footprint is broad and mostly open. Timber rail pens create the dominant rectangular and curved lines.
- Show only two or three cattle so the animals establish function without overwhelming the architecture.
- A small lean-to shelters a balance beam, standard weights, tally sticks, and one low table.
- The central yard is packed earth with hoof marks and a controlled entrance lane.
- Salt baskets, rope, a water trough, and a few ceramic containers connect animal exchange to provisioning.
- The outer fence should have several practical repairs, emphasizing repeated use rather than poverty or collapse.

### Color and Wear

Dusty ochre ground, gray-brown rails, muted cattle coats, pale salt baskets, and a restrained red textile or painted weight as the visual accent.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The pens and central lane must read as **organized exchange with unavoidable crowding**, not a farmstead.

### Reject If

- It resembles a later stone forum, medieval fair, or permanent shop arcade.
- Dozens of animals hide the site plan.
- It contains coin stalls, canvas awnings in many colors, or ornamental civic statuary.

## 6. Grain Pits

**Evidence status:** Type inference and game abstraction; exact early Roman storage arrangements remain uncertain.

### Mental Image

A dispersed storage yard contains four sealed earth-cut grain pits marked by fitted clay-and-timber caps, one open inspection mouth, several large jars, and a small thatched cover for tools and seed baskets.

### Form and Construction

- Four low circular caps form the main pattern. They are separated enough that one spoiled store would not visually imply the loss of all reserves.
- One pit may be shown in partial cutaway at the frame edge, revealing a clay-lined neck without turning the asset into a diagram.
- Each sealed mouth uses packed clay, woven matting, and a fitted timber lid weighted by stone.
- Add two large storage vessels, seed baskets raised on a timber rack, and a small open-sided thatch shelter.
- A low boundary fence and tally post indicate household accountability without implying a centralized state granary.

### Color and Wear

Packed tan earth, darker pit mouths, straw matting, red-buff ceramics, and weathered timber. Keep loose grain almost invisible; exposed piles would imply spoilage.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The repeated capped circles and jars must read as **distributed food storage**, distinct from a well or cemetery.

### Reject If

- It shows modern cylindrical silos, a massive warehouse, or open heaps of grain.
- The pits resemble graves or fire holes.
- The storage method is presented as archaeologically certain in supporting text.

## 7. Kiln and Smithy

**Evidence status:** Game abstraction combining fire-using pottery and metal craft.

### Mental Image

A clay pottery kiln and an open timber smithing shelter share a scorched outer-hill work yard filled with charcoal, unfinished vessels, a stone work block, and a large quenching jar.

### Form and Construction

- Separate the two fire processes visually. The pottery kiln is a low rounded clay mass with a stoking opening and a small vented crown.
- The smithing area is an open-sided lean-to with a compact charcoal hearth, bellows, stone or early metal work surface, tongs, and a quench vessel.
- Stack a few failed or unfinished pots near the kiln and bronze scraps near the workbench.
- Bare scorched ground creates a firebreak around both structures. Place the yard away from dense housing in every backdrop composition.
- One timber rack holds fuel above damp ground; another holds tools under cover.

### Color and Wear

Soot black, fired terracotta, warm clay, charcoal gray, dark bronze, and restrained ember orange. Smoke should be slight and vertical, not a dramatic industrial plume.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The rounded kiln plus angular shelter must read as **two linked crafts with fire risk**.

### Reject If

- It looks like a medieval blacksmith shop with horseshoes, steel anvils, stone chimney, or water wheel.
- Flames dominate the composition.
- The site is enclosed by dense buildings or paved streets.

## 8. Timber Palisade

**Evidence status:** Type inference; early hill defenses may have relied on terrain, earthworks, ditches, and timber barriers rather than a continuous formal wall.

### Mental Image

A short defended hill approach combines an earth bank, shallow outer ditch, irregular sharpened timber barrier, and narrow timber gate, creating warning and delay rather than an impregnable fortress.

### Form and Construction

- Depict a representative segment, not a complete circular enclosure.
- The palisade follows the crest of a low packed-earth bank. Posts vary slightly in diameter and height but are tightly set and braced from behind.
- A narrow double-leaf timber gate forms the focal point. Its cross-bracing and rope or bronze-pin fittings should look repairable by local craft.
- A shallow ditch and cleared strip precede the bank. A rough uphill track funnels toward the gate.
- Include a simple raised step or lookout platform inside the barrier, but no tower.
- Bundled spare stakes explain the recurring timber upkeep.

### Color and Wear

Dark weathered posts, lighter fresh repairs, dusty bank, sparse grass, and a cool shadow in the ditch.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The ditch-bank-post sequence must read as **layered warning and delay**, not merely a fence.

### Reject If

- It resembles a standardized Roman legionary camp, medieval stockade, or fantasy wall.
- It has tall towers, battlements, parapet walks, or iron portcullises.
- Every post is machined to the same shape.

## 9. Courtyard Housing

**Evidence status:** Type inference representing denser, more regular domestic organization in the City of Kings.

### Mental Image

Four low rectangular household ranges enclose most of a shared hard-packed court, with stone footings, earthen walls, mixed thatch and terracotta roofing, a covered entrance passage, and a visible runoff channel.

### Form and Construction

- The dominant silhouette is rectilinear and inward-facing, clearly different from the scattered Palatine Huts.
- Use rough tufa socles beneath timber-framed or mud-brick walls. Roofs remain pitched and modest.
- One or two roof sections use early terracotta tiles; the remainder may retain better-laid thatch. This makes technological change visible without turning the settlement uniformly tiled.
- A roofed entrance passage opens toward a narrow lane. Opposite it, a small household shrine niche or storage alcove anchors the court.
- The central court holds a hearth, loom frame, jars, and one shade tree or timber post, but remains mostly clear for work.
- A shallow stone-lined runoff channel carries water from the court toward the street side, visually justifying the drainage prerequisite.

### Color and Wear

Buff and muted red walls, gray tufa footings, straw and terracotta roofs, shaded earth court, and a narrow cool drainage line.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The enclosed central void must read as **higher-density coordinated housing**, not a palace.

### Reject If

- It resembles an imperial domus with columns, mosaics, marble atrium, fountain, or second floor.
- All roofs are pristine red tile.
- The court is ornamental rather than a household work space.

## 10. Lined Cistern

**Evidence status:** Type inference.

### Mental Image

A partly covered rectangular underground reservoir receives roof and surface runoff through terracotta spouts, with a low tufa curb, plastered interior glimpse, stone access slabs, and a small drawing canopy.

### Form and Construction

- A low rectangular perimeter distinguishes the cistern from the circular Shallow Well.
- Most of the reservoir is covered by fitted timber and stone slabs. Leave one access section open enough to reveal pale waterproof lining and dark stored water.
- A small pitched canopy protects the drawing point. It rests on four timber posts and carries two collection spouts.
- Show a settling basin at the inlet and a narrow overflow channel leading away from the structure.
- Place two ceramic jars and a wooden bucket near the access point. Keep the site orderly enough to suggest shared rules and maintenance.

### Color and Wear

Gray tufa, pale mineral lining, dark blue-green water, muted terracotta spouts, and weathered timber.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The rectangular covered basin and inlet system must read as **stored and managed water**, not another well.

### Reject If

- It contains an aqueduct arcade, pressure fountain, vaulted imperial chamber, or ornamental pool.
- The full reservoir is exposed to dirt and animals.
- It has elaborate masonry unrelated to a practical neighborhood work.

## 11. Raised Granary

**Evidence status:** Type inference emphasizing the game's spoilage, supervision, and theft mechanics.

### Mental Image

Two long timber storehouses stand on short tufa piers above a swept yard, reached by removable stairs and watched from a small tally shelter, with narrow vents and controlled loading space.

### Form and Construction

- The main granary is a rectangular timber structure lifted approximately half a meter above ground on several low stone piers.
- A smaller companion store repeats the form at a slight angle, creating a supervised compound rather than one oversized warehouse.
- Raised floors, narrow ventilation gaps below the eaves, and generous roof overhang explain reduced damp and spoilage.
- Use mixed heavy thatch and restrained terracotta edging or tile on the main roof. Keep walls timber plank or tightly daubed frame.
- A short removable stair or ramp leads to the loading door. Add a weighing beam, sealed jars, tally board, and waist-high perimeter fence.
- The yard should be dry, level, and visibly maintained, with no loose grain exposed.

### Color and Wear

Warm dark timber, gray piers, muted straw, a few terracotta caps, and pale swept earth.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The visible air gap below the floors must read as **raised protected storage**.

### Reject If

- It resembles a later brick horreum, medieval barn, or modern stilt house.
- Grain spills openly from doors and carts.
- The building is monumental or highly decorated.

## 12. Stone Gate

**Evidence status:** Type inference with explicit chronological caution. Early stone walls and gates are disputed and later reinforced wall remains should not be projected wholesale into the regal period.

### Mental Image

A compact gray-tufa gate interrupts an older earth bank: squared but uneven stone jambs support a heavy timber lintel and upper fighting platform, while a bronze-fitted double gate controls a rutted road, toll bench, and defended approach.

### Form and Construction

- Show only a short wall segment on either side, merging into an earth rampart so the asset does not imply a fully reconstructed fourth-century circuit.
- Build the lower gate from courses of dark gray tufa blocks that vary slightly in height and finish.
- Use a heavy timber lintel rather than a confident monumental arch. This is both visually distinctive and more cautious for the game's 650-509 BC phase.
- The double timber leaves have diagonal bracing, bronze pins or straps, and a stout removable closing beam visible on the inner side.
- A modest timber platform above or behind the opening provides lookout and missile cover. It must remain subordinate to the gate mass.
- The roadway narrows through the opening. Wheel ruts, a toll bench, standard weights, and one sheltered guard position explain the trade and order benefits.
- Add staged stone blocks and timber scaffolding in the separate under-construction version because this is a two-season project.

### Color and Wear

Dark gray tufa, earth-brown rampart, near-black gate timber, green-brown bronze fittings, and a pale road worn through the opening.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The narrow controlled opening between stone masses must read as **defense, toll collection, and ceremony together**.

### Reject If

- It resembles a triumphal arch, imperial gatehouse, Greek acropolis, medieval castle, or legionary camp entrance.
- It has high towers, crenellations, concrete vaults, or white marble.
- The wall extends so far that the gate reads as a full city circuit rather than one controlled approach.

## 13. Cloaca Works

**Evidence status:** Evidence-led hydraulic form with a game-scale cutaway. The late sixth-century work is represented as a flat-covered double channel rather than the later monumental vaulted sewer.

### Mental Image

A long diagonal slice through the Forum valley shows two parallel tufa-lined drainage channels beneath heavy overlapping capstones. One end remains open to the brown stream; the other disappears beneath a newly raised packed-earth civic surface, with feeder cuts and spoil banks explaining how the wet valley is being made usable.

### Form and Construction

- The dominant silhouette is low and elongated, continuing the Drainage Ditch family rather than becoming a freestanding monument.
- Expose one short cutaway end containing two narrow channels divided by a central wall of roughly squared gray cappellaccio blocks.
- Cover most of both channels with two parallel rows of broad flat stone slabs. Keep the top nearly level and partly buried beneath packed fill.
- Let one upstream section remain open and brush-revetted so the transition from managed stream to covered work is visible.
- Add two small feeder drains, a temporary timber lifting frame, stacked capstones, wooden rollers, baskets, and spoil heaps. These should explain the labor without turning the asset into a construction scene crowded with workers.
- A short strip of firm packed-earth surface crosses above the covered section. Sparse stakes can mark the future Forum boundary.

### Color and Wear

Dark gray porous tufa, wet umber soil, shallow brown water, pale fresh fill, and weathered lifting timbers. The covered surface should look newly consolidated rather than paved in marble or regular flagstone.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The paired channel mouths, long capstone strip, and dry ground above must read as **drainage creating civic land**.

### Reject If

- It uses a barrel vault, concrete arch, brick lining, or the monumental imperial outfall.
- It resembles an aqueduct, canal lock, bath conduit, or underground fantasy tunnel.
- The channels are clean, dry, or large enough for carts and crowds.
- It loses the double-channel form or reads as an ordinary stone road.

## 14. Temple of Jupiter

**Evidence status:** Evidence-led massing based on the late sixth-century Capitoline foundations and Tuscanic plan; upper-wall colors and decorative arrangement remain reconstructive.

### Mental Image

A broad, nearly square temple rises on a high dark-tufa podium above the Capitoline slope. A single frontal stair leads to a deep shaded pronaos with widely spaced Tuscan columns, while painted terracotta roof edges and one restrained roof quadriga provide the monument's color and regal identity.

### Form and Construction

- The building must look wider and deeper than a Greek peripteral temple. Use a near-square footprint and a heavy podium occupying most of the asset.
- Place the only principal stair on the front. The remaining podium sides stay high and inaccessible.
- A deep front porch occupies roughly half the visible depth. Show three frontal column bays with additional columns receding into shadow rather than a continuous Greek colonnade on every side.
- Suggest the three rear cellae through three dark doorways or a tripartite rear mass, with the center division wider than the flanking two.
- Use gray cappellaccio foundations and podium blocks, timber roof structure, pale earthen wall surfaces, and a broad low-pitched tiled roof.
- Terracotta revetment plaques, antefixes, and ridge ornaments carry muted red, cream, black, and ochre pattern. Keep one small terracotta quadriga centered on the roof ridge as a recognizable accent, not an oversized statue.
- Add a cleared forecourt with a low altar and a few votive vessels. Do not surround the site with later marble monuments.

### Color and Wear

Dark gray tufa podium, warm pale walls, muted terracotta roof, painted architectural clay in restrained red, cream, ochre, and black, and deep porch shadow. No white marble sheen.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The high podium, frontal stair, very deep porch, broad roof, and terracotta crown must read as **the archaic Capitoline state temple**, not a generic classical temple.

### Reject If

- It uses Corinthian or Ionic orders, a marble pediment, freestanding sculpture garden, concrete, dome, or imperial stair plaza.
- It copies the Parthenon or wraps an even Greek colonnade around all four sides.
- The roof quadriga becomes the main mass or appears bronze.
- The temple is narrow, vertically exaggerated, pristine white, or detached from the Capitoline terrain.

## 15. Forum Market

**Evidence status:** Game abstraction. The asset represents supervised exchange on the reclaimed Forum surface without claiming a recovered plan for one royal-period market complex.

### Mental Image

Three orderly rows of timber stalls define an open packed-earth market court over the drained valley. Scales, standard measures, grain jars, salt baskets, pottery, and a public tally shelter make oversight visible while leaving broad paths for bargaining and movement.

### Form and Construction

- Keep most of the footprint open. The primary geometry is a U-shaped arrangement of low timber stall roofs around a central bargaining lane.
- Use posts on rough stone pads, simple plank counters, removable shutters, and mixed thatch with limited terracotta edging. Do not create permanent masonry shop blocks.
- A slightly larger public shelter at one corner contains a balance beam, standard stone or bronze weights, tally boards, and sealed measures.
- Divide goods into a few legible zones: jars and baskets, folded cloth or hides, pottery, and a small produce table. Avoid coins, luxury-shop displays, or food-service counters.
- Mark the court with drainage grooves and a raised packed-earth surface. One visible edge may reveal the capstones of the Cloaca Works beneath it.
- Add tethering posts and a single handcart, but no dense livestock pen; the earlier Cattle Market already carries that silhouette.

### Color and Wear

Pale packed earth, gray-brown timber, straw roofs, buff and red pottery, faded textile accents, and dark standard weights. The site should feel regulated but provisional.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The repeated low stall roofs, open central lane, and weighing shelter must read as **organized public exchange**.

### Reject If

- It resembles Trajan's Market, a basilica, a colonnaded imperial forum, a medieval bazaar, or a modern farmers market.
- Stone arcades, marble paving, canvas canopies in many colors, glass, or coin counters dominate the scene.
- Goods and people obscure the open civic surface.
- It reads as another cattle pen or one private shop.

## 16. Workshop Quarter

**Evidence status:** Game abstraction grounded in archaic ceramic, architectural-terracotta, and metalworking production. It combines several hazardous crafts into one legible district asset.

### Mental Image

Four low open-front work sheds ring a broad scorched yard: a paired pottery kiln, a terracotta-finishing bench, a charcoal metal hearth, and covered material racks. Water jars, firebreaks, stacked clay, molds, and bronze scraps show concentrated output under practical supervision.

### Form and Construction

- Build a loose compound rather than one factory. Low timber-and-daub sheds occupy three sides while the open yard remains the dominant void.
- Two clay kilns of different sizes create the first silhouette anchor. Their stoking mouths and vented crowns remain distinct from bread ovens.
- The longest shed contains racks of drying tiles and vessels, clay molds, pigments, and a broad finishing table.
- A separate smaller shelter contains a compact charcoal hearth, leather bellows, stone work block, crucibles, and dark-bronze tools. Keep it visibly separated from stored timber and clay by bare earth.
- Add a lined water trough, large ceramic quenching jars, stacked fuel under a lean-to, clay settling pits, and a low boundary ditch serving as a firebreak.
- Finished objects should remain modest: roof tiles, antefix blanks, vessels, fittings, and a few bronze tools. No weapon arsenal or mass-production line.

### Color and Wear

Terracotta red, pale raw clay, soot black, dark bronze, weathered timber, muted straw, and broad patches of scorched brown earth. Use only a faint ember glow and almost no smoke.

### Required Read at Game Scale

The paired kilns, long drying shed, separate metal hearth, and open firebreak yard must read as **concentrated urban craft with greater output and danger**.

### Reject If

- It resembles a medieval guild street, Roman imperial factory, industrial foundry, arsenal, or fantasy forge.
- It contains steel anvils, horseshoes, chimneys, water wheels, cranes, smokestacks, or large furnaces.
- Every craft is enclosed in one building.
- Flames, smoke, workers, or finished weapons dominate the site plan.

## Approval Sequence

1. Produce a grayscale silhouette sketch of Palatine Huts at 256 x 256.
2. Approve cluster arrangement and recognizability before materials.
3. Produce one fully rendered 2048 x 2048 Palatine Huts master.
4. Test it at 96 x 96 on the olive city map and at mobile scale.
5. Lock camera, lighting, palette, edge treatment, and ground contact.
6. Sketch the remaining eleven assets in grayscale as one contact sheet.
7. Approve the entire silhouette family before any additional final rendering.

## Research Basis and Uncertainty

- The Palatine hut brief follows the Parco archeologico del Colosseo descriptions of the Cermalus settlement: simple oval or modest rectangular single-room structures, timber posts, wattle-and-daub walls, straw roofs, central hearths, and occasional entrance porticoes.
- The Timber Shrine deliberately emphasizes an open sacred boundary because early Italic worship need not be represented by a complete monumental temple. Monumental Etruscan-influenced temples belong to the later Temple of Jupiter asset.
- The gate and fortification briefs remain cautious. Early hill settlements may have relied on terrain and earthworks; much of the impressive surviving squared-tufa circuit belongs to later rebuilding.
- Wells, cisterns, markets, storage, craft yards, and domestic compounds are functional visual reconstructions. Their exact layouts should not be described as recovered plans of named early Roman buildings.
- The Cloaca Works brief follows the Sovrintendenza Capitolina's identification of the archaic work as two parallel channels with a central wall and flat block covering. Later concrete and vaulted phases are excluded.
- The Temple of Jupiter brief follows the Capitoline Museums' late sixth-century foundation evidence and Tuscanic reconstruction: a high podium, frontal stair, deep pronaos, three cellae, gray cappellaccio foundations, and terracotta roof sculpture.
- The Forum Market and Workshop Quarter remain declared game abstractions. The former uses the packed-earth civic surface attested for the archaic Forum; the latter combines archaeologically attested ceramic, architectural-terracotta, and metalworking activity without claiming one recovered compound plan.

Primary visual references should be archived with each approved master so future assets do not drift toward imperial, medieval, or fantasy Rome.
