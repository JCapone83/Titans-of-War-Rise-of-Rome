# Act VIII Balance Report

## Scope

Act VIII covers **133-49 BC**. It runs from the 133-121 BC transition through seven playable councils at 121, 91, 88, 82, 78, 63, and 49 BC. It ends at the civil-war threshold. It does not decide Caesar's crossing, simulate the civil war, or prescribe the constitutional settlement reserved for Act IX.

## Mechanical Claim

The act keeps twelve pressures separate: citizenship integration, Italian claims, personal army risk, Senate command control, emergency precedent, land-title disputes, street violence, court capacity, archive integrity, demobilization capacity, lawful public channels, and urban fire response. No single legitimacy value can hide a failure in command, citizenship, records, or city operations.

Five dated civic works convert policy into operating capacity:

- Land and Census Registry
- Italian Citizenship Registers
- Forum Courts and Assembly Routes
- Tabularium State Archive
- Watch and Service Stations

Each consumes seasonal public capacity and materials, enforces its dated availability and prerequisite, and adds a recurring operating charge after completion. The Tabularium is presented with explicit uncertainty about its ancient name and the use of every room.

All three campaigns reach turn 48 at 49 BC with `republic-strain-complete`, make seven declared council decisions, and record no skipped actions. The first two execute seven declared project actions. The bounded-command strategy executes six, then preserves its remaining resources rather than starting a work it cannot complete before the civil-war threshold. The campaigns preserve distinct citizenship settlements and public-works portfolios.

## Historical Boundaries

The context notes distinguish participant evidence from later connected narratives. Appian and Plutarch are used with explicit distance warnings; Cicero and Caesar are treated as partisan participants rather than neutral transcripts. Legal fragments, inscriptions, municipal evidence, and archaeology constrain claims where the literary record is incomplete.

The final council exposes mutual demobilization, a senatorial ultimatum, and a single restoring command as different institutional risks. The engine stops before assigning victory or inevitability to any civil-war branch.
