# Batch 5 Structure Concept Bible

## Purpose and locked production rules

This is the pre-render contract for the seven mechanically stable Early Republic forms in Batch 5. It preserves the shared art direction: transparent alpha, no labels or scenery, warm neutral light from upper left, soft contact shadow, and the locked three-quarter orthographic camera at 35 degrees clockwise from the facade and 28 degrees above ground. Each asset must show the whole site with 8–10 percent clear space and remain legible at 96 × 96 pixels. The four future assets are briefs, not runtime mappings; they remain absent from `BUILDING_ART` until inspected alpha files are approved.

Evidence language is deliberate. **Evidence-led** means supported by surviving traces or well-attested material practice. **Type inference** means a plausible functional reconstruction where no exact early plan survives. **Game abstraction** combines related facilities into one readable asset. None of these briefs claims that a surviving image or excavation preserves the exact proposed arrangement.

## 1. Comitium

**Evidence status:** Type inference with evidence-led civic topography and early public-space practice. The exact Early Republic appearance of the Comitium is uncertain; this is a bounded game abstraction of a meeting and proclamation place, not a claim to reconstruct a single excavated phase.

**Historical and gameplay purpose:** Represent an open civic institution where citizens gather, magistrates speak, and public consent becomes visible. Mechanically it is a unique Forum civic work that raises order and trade, so the image must communicate assembly, access, and controlled public space rather than private housing or a temple.

**Footprint and massing:** Use a compact rectangular or slightly fan-shaped packed-earth court, approximately 18 by 14 meters in the implied site scale. A low irregular tufa retaining curb encloses the court on three sides. Place a low timber speaking platform on the far edge, no taller than one story and only a little above the court. Add two short post-and-rope entry lines and a narrow side stair or ramp. Keep the center open; do not fill it with a roofed hall.

**Materials:** Rough local tufa curb and steps; packed earth with worn pale footpaths; dark timber posts, plank platform, rope, and a small amount of terracotta edging. No white marble, dressed imperial concrete, or polished monumental paving.

**Functional details:** Show the platform, a low boundary, a magistrate's seat or chest, and one bronze notice or tally board as tiny scale cues. Include a drainage groove at the edge because the Forum low ground remains managed rather than perfectly dry. No crowd is required; at most use two cut-off silhouettes only if they do not reduce the alpha silhouette.

**Locked composition and silhouette:** Three-quarter view from the entry corner, with the open court in front, platform at rear right, and curb leading diagonally toward the viewer. The empty central void is the identifying silhouette at 96 pixels; the raised platform and two entry posts must survive reduction. Keep the camera, light, shadow, and transparent ground treatment identical to the Palatine Huts master.

**Progression:** This is the civic advance from the Forum Market's exchange court: the preceding tier organizes goods and stalls, while the Comitium organizes speech, decision, and public access. It may share rough tufa and packed earth but must be more deliberately bounded and less cluttered.

**Rejection criteria:** Reject any closed basilica, amphitheater, marble senate house, temple podium, triumphal stair, tiled imperial plaza, or medieval market square. Reject a completely empty patch with no platform or boundary, and reject a dense crowd whose bodies become the asset's subject.

## 2. Temple of Saturn and Aerarium

**Evidence status:** Type inference anchored to the long-attested association of Saturn with the public treasury and to early Italic temple materials. The familiar monumental Temple of Saturn belongs to later rebuilding phases; this asset must not present the later surviving facade as certain Early Republic evidence.

**Historical and gameplay purpose:** Visualize a sacred treasury where ritual legitimacy and guarded public reserves meet. Mechanically it is a unique Forum treasury work requiring the Comitium, so the structure should read as a controlled institutional store with a cult focus, not as a generic rich temple.

**Footprint and massing:** Use a narrow rectangular precinct about 16 by 11 meters, with a low three-step tufa podium and a small timber-and-terracotta temple cella set toward the rear. Give the facade four stout timber or rough-stone supports under a shallow pitched roof, but keep the elevation low and compact. Put a separately legible lockable treasury chamber or stone-lined storeroom behind and slightly to one side, connected by a low wall. The rear chamber must not become a second temple.

**Materials:** Rough gray tufa foundation and steps; timber columns or posts; painted or plain terracotta roof tiles; dark bronze door fittings; plastered earth or early mud-brick wall surfaces. Restrained red and ochre accents are allowed, but no marble entablature or Corinthian order.

**Functional details:** Show a sealed chest or barred doorway within the side treasury, a small altar or offering table at the front, and a short precinct boundary with one controlled entrance. The treasury should be visibly protected by supervision and access geometry, not by a fantasy vault or modern bank door.

**Locked composition and silhouette:** Three-quarter view from the front-left, with the low podium and four-support facade forming the main stepped silhouette, the side treasury volume visible behind it, and a soft shadow beneath the full precinct. At 96 pixels the viewer must distinguish temple roof, podium, and attached storage chamber. Retain clear space on every side and the approved orthographic camera.

**Progression:** This follows the Comitium: public speech and consent become durable reserves administered under religious and civic authority. It should be more formal and vertically ordered than the open meeting court, while remaining materially modest and smaller than a later stone temple.

**Rejection criteria:** Reject the extant imperial-looking Temple of Saturn facade, marble columns, high classical podium, pediment crowded with sculpture, domed treasury, triumphal arch, or a freestanding palace vault. Reject a temple with no visible storage or controlled access, and reject a generic shrine that cannot read as a public treasury.

## 3. Circuit Fortification

**Evidence status:** Type inference and game abstraction. Early Rome's defensive phases and the dating of impressive surviving walls are complex; this image combines a coordinated hill boundary, gates, and earthworks without claiming to depict the complete Servian Wall.

**Historical and gameplay purpose:** Show the shift from a local timber palisade and isolated Stone Gate to a negotiated defensive system joining approaches and high ground. Mechanically it is a major defense work with multi-season cost, so the asset must communicate continuity, labor, and controlled access rather than an invulnerable later city wall.

**Footprint and massing:** Use an angled fragment of circuit, approximately 28 by 18 meters in implied site scale, with two connected low wall runs, one squat gate tower, an earthen rampart, and a shallow ditch. The visible wall should curve or kink around the site rather than form a complete ring. Keep the wall one to two human heights above the rampart; the gate tower is only modestly higher and has a timber parapet.

**Materials:** Rough irregular tufa and local stone facing over packed earth; timber braces and gate leaves; dark soil rampart; sparse bronze fittings. Use visible patching and uneven courses. No poured concrete, dressed imperial ashlar, crenellated medieval battlements, or uniform modern masonry.

**Functional details:** The gate has a double-leaf timber door, a narrow controlled passage, a side stair, and a watch platform. Include the ditch, an approach track, and one repair stack of stone to make sustained maintenance legible. The circuit fragment must leave the frame open so it does not imply a total city enclosure.

**Locked composition and silhouette:** Three-quarter view from outside the approach, with the gate at the front-right focal point, wall receding diagonally left, and rampart rising behind. The gate opening, wall kink, and earthen slope must remain distinct at 96 pixels. Use the same upper-left light and direct contact shadow; do not add banners, soldiers, or landscape beyond the transparent ground patch.

**Progression:** This advances from the Timber Palisade and Stone Gate: warning and a single controlled approach become linked defenses with a shared maintenance burden. It must visibly retain timber gates and earthworks even while stone and organization increase.

**Rejection criteria:** Reject a complete circular city wall, medieval castle, legionary camp with regular towers, imperial fort, monumental gate arch, battlement crown, or fantasy fortress. Reject a wall so low that it reads as a garden boundary, and reject a pristine stone ring with no earthwork or repair evidence.

## 4. Ordered Street Courts

**Evidence status:** Type inference and game abstraction from early urban drainage, courtyard housing, and the general development of aligned streets. No exact single Early Republic block plan is asserted.

**Historical and gameplay purpose:** Depict managed residential density after open household compounds and rough drainage. Mechanically this tier increases shelter, sanitation, and order, so the asset must read as several households organized around shared access and runoff rather than as one wealthy villa.

**Footprint and massing:** Use a long rectangular block about 24 by 16 meters, divided into two or three connected one-story courtyard courts by a clear lane. Put low wattle-and-daub or mud-brick rooms around each court, with slightly different roof lines. The lane runs diagonally through the site and joins a shallow stone-edged drain. Keep the courts open enough to be readable from above; no building may exceed one story.

**Materials:** Packed earth, rough tufa footings, timber framing, wattle-and-daub, thatch mixed with a few terracotta tiles on the most public eaves, wooden shutters, and clay vessels. Use repaired, varied surfaces rather than a uniform plaster block.

**Functional details:** Show thresholds, a shared water jar point, small domestic work benches, a covered drain crossing, and narrow gates controlling the lane. Use no private colonnade or fountain; the civic value is alignment, maintenance, and shared sanitation.

**Locked composition and silhouette:** Three-quarter view from the lane entrance, with the diagonal access lane and two court voids forming a strong broken silhouette. At 96 pixels the viewer must see a dense but low block with visible internal openings, not a single roof. Keep the asset fully isolated on transparent alpha with the standard shadow.

**Progression:** This follows Courtyard Housing and the Cloaca Works: private households gain a more ordered shared street and court system linked to public drainage. The visual progression is alignment and service access, not luxury or height.

**Rejection criteria:** Reject a Roman imperial insula, apartment tower, marble villa, colonnaded palace, modern street grid, paved boulevard, or a single isolated courtyard house. Reject roofs that erase the court openings and reject any sewer shown as a monumental aqueduct.

## 5. Public Cistern Network

**Evidence status:** Type inference and game abstraction from lined water storage, covered reservoirs, and supervised public distribution. The image is not an excavated network plan and must not imply later aqueduct engineering.

**Historical and gameplay purpose:** Show several linked, maintained water stores that turn local provision into a civic obligation. Mechanically this tier expands water and sanitation capacity and precedes Contracted Craft Yards, so the asset should communicate dependable access, inspection, and distributed service.

**Footprint and massing:** Use a compact service compound about 22 by 15 meters containing two low rectangular stone-lined cistern mouths at different elevations, a covered storage chamber with a shallow timber-and-tile roof, and a visible settling basin. Connect them with one open or covered channel that is visibly small and gravity-fed. Keep all vertical mass low; the reservoirs and their covers are the dominant forms.

**Materials:** Rough tufa blocks, waterproofed lime or clay lining indicated only at openings, timber maintenance hatch, terracotta channel pieces, packed earth, and a few bronze or wooden fittings. Avoid long arches, pressure pipes, or polished stone tanks.

**Functional details:** Include a stepped inspection access, removable cover slabs, a tap or controlled outlet, a drainage spillway, and two public jars. The linked components must be legible as a network without drawing an unrealistic maze of channels.

**Locked composition and silhouette:** Three-quarter view from the public approach, with the near cistern mouth and cover at front-left, the second reservoir behind-right, and the channel creating a clear diagonal. At 96 pixels the two storage masses and connecting line must survive as separate shapes. Use transparent alpha and the locked light/shadow treatment.

**Progression:** This follows the Lined Cistern: one protected store becomes several supervised stores with distribution and maintenance. It should look more connected and administratively complex, not simply like a larger single tank.

**Rejection criteria:** Reject an aqueduct, fountain plaza, Roman bath, concrete barrel vault, pressurized plumbing, modern reservoir, or a single circular well. Reject exposed water surfaces that make the asset look like a pond, and reject a channel network too fine to read at map scale.

## 6. Public Granary

**Evidence status:** Type inference and game abstraction grounded in raised storage, pest and moisture control, and supervised communal reserves. No exact state granary plan is claimed for this period.

**Historical and gameplay purpose:** Make public food reserve and distribution visible as an institution rather than a cluster of household pits. Mechanically this tier increases grain production, order, and upkeep obligations, so it needs a clear storehouse, controlled access, and handling yard.

**Footprint and massing:** Use a rectangular compound about 20 by 14 meters. The principal granary is a long timber store, approximately 12 by 6 meters, raised on six squat rough-stone piers with a shallow gabled roof. Add a smaller lock room and a fenced loading yard offset to the front. The floor must be visibly elevated but not dramatically tall; the site remains Early Republic and one story.

**Materials:** Rough tufa or local stone piers, timber frame and plank siding, packed earth, thatch or restrained terracotta roof tiles, wicker grain baskets, clay sealing jars, and a timber fence. Use weathering, patched boards, and a few dark ventilation gaps.

**Functional details:** Show a short ladder or removable stair, a locked door, raised loading platform, covered sacks or baskets, a tally board, and a narrow drainage strip beneath the floor. The store's controlled threshold and open work yard should explain public supervision without adding modern signage.

**Locked composition and silhouette:** Three-quarter view from the loading-yard corner, with the elevated long granary dominating rear-left, piers visible below, and the fence and ladder leading toward the viewer. At 96 pixels the raised gap, long roof, and fenced yard must remain distinct. Use the standard transparent ground and soft contact shadow.

**Progression:** This follows Raised Granary: household-scale elevated storage becomes a supervised reserve with larger capacity, controlled issue, and an attached handling yard. Preserve the raised-floor silhouette while increasing length and organization.

**Rejection criteria:** Reject a monumental marble horreum, multi-story warehouse, silo, modern barn, military supply depot with imperial architecture, or a granary resting directly on the ground. Reject an empty civic courtyard with no elevated store and reject excessive sacks that obscure the building silhouette.

## 7. Contracted Craft Yards

**Evidence status:** Game abstraction combining plausible pottery, metal, stone, and timber production into one supervised Early Republic work compound. It is not a claim that one historical yard contained every craft shown.

**Historical and gameplay purpose:** Show magistrates contracting specialized makers while preserving household labor and civic oversight. Mechanically this tier increases bronze and stone output, readiness, and trade while adding sanitation and treasury upkeep, so the image must make productive concentration and fire risk immediately legible.

**Footprint and massing:** Use a broad irregular compound about 26 by 18 meters with four zones around a central firebreak yard: a paired pottery kiln shelter at rear-left, a separate small metal hearth at rear-right, a long open-sided drying shed along the back, and a stone-cutting bench near the entrance. Low fences and work surfaces divide zones without enclosing every craft in a building. Leave a clear access lane and a water jar station connected visually to the Public Cistern Network.

**Materials:** Rough stone kiln bases, brick or clay kiln domes, timber posts, thatch or tile eaves, packed earth, dark charcoal, bronze tools, pale stone blanks, terracotta sherds, and woven drying racks. Keep smoke staining localized and palette restrained; no industrial steel or medieval forge hall.

**Functional details:** Show one pottery wheel or work table, stacked unfired vessels, a small anvil and charcoal hearth, stone blocks with chips, drying racks, a tool chest, a contractual tally board, and a low firebreak wall. Use only a faint controlled smoke cue if it survives alpha inspection. The yards must look supervised but actively productive.

**Locked composition and silhouette:** Three-quarter view from the entry lane, with the paired kiln forms as the strongest rear-left mass, drying shed behind, hearth and anvil at rear-right, and the open firebreak yard centered. At 96 pixels the two kiln domes, long shed, and open central gap must be distinct. Maintain the locked camera, upper-left daylight, transparent background, and soft shadow.

**Progression:** This follows Workshop Quarter: dispersed concentrated craft becomes a contracted civic production yard with clearer zoning, water access, accounting, and fire control. It should be more organized and productive without becoming a permanent imperial manufactory.

**Rejection criteria:** Reject a single enclosed factory, medieval guildhall, industrial smoke-stack, modern workshop, palace courtyard, fantasy forge, or a generic market. Reject uncontrolled flames, dense smoke, or a composition where every craft is hidden inside one building; the separated hazardous zones and central firebreak are required.

## Approval and runtime gate

The three existing alpha assets are integrated only as provenance-recorded game interpretations: `public/images/buildings/comitium-v1.png`, `public/images/buildings/saturn-treasury-v1.png`, and `public/images/buildings/circuit-fortification-v1.png`. Their filenames establish delivery identity, not archaeological certainty. They must continue to pass transparent-edge, camera, 96-pixel silhouette, and map-scale inspection.

The four future forms—Ordered Street Courts, Public Cistern Network, Public Granary, and Contracted Craft Yards—remain unrendered and intentionally absent from runtime art mapping. A future integration may add each exact building ID only after its alpha file is provenance-recorded, inspected at full size and 96 pixels, checked against the rejection criteria, and verified on desktop and mobile. No brief authorizes a simulation, cost, unlock, council, outcome, interface, dependency, or existing art change.
